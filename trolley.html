Philosophy

The Trolley Problem

A famous ethical dilemma that challenges our moral decision-making.

Key Concepts

  • Neuroplasticity: The biological ability of the nervous system to change its activity and reorganize its structure, functions, or connections in response to internal or external stimuli throughout a person's entire life.
  • Synaptic Plasticity: The cellular basis of learning where repetitive stimulation physically adds new neurotransmitter receptors to a membrane, thereby strengthening the neural connection.
  • Maladaptive Plasticity: A process where the brain "learns the wrong lesson" by wiring itself to become more efficient at processing negative inputs, such as chronic stress or persistent pain signals.
  • Cognitive Reserve: The brain's internal defense system against age-related decline, which is built up through lifelong learning, social interaction, and challenging mental workouts.
  • Brain-Derived Neurotrophic Factor (BDNF): A key protein triggered by aerobic exercise that acts as "miracle growth" for the brain, promoting cell development and strengthening neural pathways.

Big Question

"If our daily habits literally rewrite our neural architecture, are we the passive products of our environment or the active architects of our own biological identity?"

Questions to Explore

  • How does sleep act as the critical biological window for the brain to consolidate short-term experiences into permanent neural connections?
  • In what ways can targeted therapies, such as mirror therapy or vagus nerve stimulation, force a damaged brain to preserve or regain lost functions?
  • Why is aerobic exercise considered more effective for brain health than simple mental repetition in preventing cognitive functions from beginning to deteriorate?
