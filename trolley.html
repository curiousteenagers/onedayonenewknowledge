Philosophy

The Trolley Problem

A famous ethical dilemma that challenges our moral decision-making.

Key Concepts

  • Diffusion of Responsibility: A psychological phenomenon where individuals in a crowd feel less pressure to act because they subconsciously assume someone else will take charge [1]. The source notes this "waters down" the personal drive to intervene [1].
  • Medial Prefrontal Cortex (mPFC): The specific neurological region responsible for preparing a person to help others [2]. Functional MRI studies show this area literally "powers down" when an individual is surrounded by a crowd [2].
  • Kin Selection: An evolutionary strategy where individuals help those with shared genetics to ensure the survival of their DNA line [3]. While mentioned as a driver for altruism, the source doesn't explain the specific genetic calculations involved [3].
  • Reciprocal Altruism: The pragmatic biological concept of helping someone today with the expectation that they, or the community, will return the favor in the future [4]. It serves as an evolutionary "insurance policy" for survival [4].

Vocabulary

Indifferent (C1) Not thinking about or interested in someone or something [5].
Hesitant (C1) If you are hesitant, you do not do something immediately because you are nervous or not certain [5].
Contradiction (C1) A big difference between two things that are said or offered as evidence [6].
Pragmatic (C2) Solving problems in a sensible way that suits the conditions that really exist [4].
Dampen (C2) To make something, such as a feeling or a biological response, less strong [2].

Big Question

"Is the bystander effect a moral failing of modern society or a deep-seated neurological survival mechanism that humans must learn to override?" [2, 7]

Questions to Explore

  • How did the 2019 security camera study in three countries challenge the **"myth of apathy"** originally popularized by the Kitty Genovese case? [6]
  • In what way does the internal "tug-of-war" between **personal distress** and **sympathy** determine whether a witness freezes or acts? [2]
  • What are the three specific steps a victim can take to **"shatter"** the diffusion of responsibility and force a bystander's brain to take action? [4, 8]
