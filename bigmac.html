Big Mac Index

A surprisingly simple way to compare the value of currencies around the world.

Key Concepts

  • Purchasing Power Parity (PPP): An economic theory suggesting that in a balanced economy, a currency converted to another should buy the exact same amount of goods globally [1]. The Big Mac Index uses this as its foundation to test if exchange rates are in their "correct parity" [1], [2]. It assumes there should be no huge disparity in the cost of basic goods across borders [1].
  • Exchange Rates: The official rates used to convert local burger prices into US dollars for direct comparison [3]. While the index highlights how these rates make a currency seem overvalued or undervalued, it does not explain the market forces that set these official values [4]. They serve as the mathematical bridge between local costs and the American baseline [3].
  • Gross Domestic Product (GDP): A measure of a country's economic output used to refine the "raw" data by factoring in local wealth [5]. It accounts for the reality that poorer countries naturally have lower labor and real estate costs, which should theoretically make burgers cheaper [5]. Adjusting for this can reveal a much more staggering level of currency overvaluation in volatile economies [5].
  • Non-tradable Factors: Local elements like taxes, labor laws, and government price controls that influence a burger's price but cannot be traded on international markets [6]. These costs often create "blind spots" because the index assumes the product exists in a vacuum [6]. They explain why a high price might actually reflect crushing domestic burdens rather than currency strength [7].

Big Question

"Is the Big Mac Index a legitimate scientific compass for navigating global macroeconomics or just a simplified tool for entertainment?"

Questions to Explore

  • How do **non-tradable factors** like local rent and labor laws create "blind spots" in currency valuation? [6].
  • What does a high price in a **developing nation** imply about the burger's status as a "luxury experience"? [6].
  • How does **GDP adjustment** drastically change the perception of an "overvalued" currency in volatile economies? [5].
