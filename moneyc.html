Why Do Currencies Gain and Lose Value?

he economic forces that determine the value of money around the world.

Key Concepts

  • Exchange Rate: The value of one currency for the purpose of conversion to another, primarily determined by the global forces of supply and demand [1].
  • Currency Appreciation vs. Depreciation: Appreciation occurs when a home currency increases in value and buys more foreign currency, whereas depreciation signifies a decrease in value and a loss of international purchasing power [1, 2].
  • Dollar Dominance: The US dollar acts as the "grease in the wheels" of global trade, involved in 88% of all foreign exchange trades and anchoring 58% of the world's financial wealth [2, 3].
  • Economic Levers: Central banks influence currency value by adjusting interest rates (which act as magnets for capital), managing inflation, and maintaining the trade balance [3, 4].
  • Safe Havens: During global crises, investors panic and rush toward historically stable and neutral currencies, such as the Swiss Franc [5].

Vocabulary

Asymmetry (C2) A situation in which the two halves or parts of something are not the same or equal in size or shape [3].
Fluctuate (C1) To change, especially continuously and between one level or thing and another [3].
Plummet (C1) To fall very quickly and suddenly [5].
Unwavering (C2) Never changing or becoming weaker [6].
Yield (C1) The total amount of profit or income from an investment [4].

Big Question

"Is a national currency a concrete reflection of economic math or merely a fragile collective barometer of global trust and confidence?" [6, 7]

Questions to Explore

  • How do central banks use **interest rates** to attract global capital and cause rapid currency appreciation? [4]
  • Why does the overwhelming **dominance of the dollar** create a deep asymmetry that sends shockwaves through every other economy? [2, 3]
  • In what ways does a weak currency cause **imported inflation**, directly reducing the living standards of citizens? [7, 8]
